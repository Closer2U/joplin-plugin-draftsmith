# Ugly Draft → Polished Story: A Reusable Editing System

Designed for ~3,000-word chapters, short stories, and fanfiction drafts with bracket notes, synonym piles, missing sections, unfinished sentences, and placeholders.

## Core Principle

Do **not** edit an ugly first draft as if it is prose. Treat it as a **scene packet**: part manuscript, part outline, part note dump, part emotional fossil record.

Your job is not to make every sentence better immediately. Your job is to move the draft through controlled states:

1. **Raw Draft** — messy, interrupted, full of notes.
2. **Legible Draft** — every sentence is complete enough to read.
3. **Scene Draft** — the scene has purpose, pressure, movement, and character logic.
4. **Voice Draft** — characters sound and act specifically.
5. **Prose Draft** — rhythm, word choice, imagery, and cuts.
6. **Reader Draft** — the story can be read without seeing the scaffolding.
7. **Polished Draft** — final line-level tightening.

You are not allowed to do phase 6 work while the draft is still in phase 1.

# The Seven-Pass System

## Pass 0 — The Quarantine Pass: Separate Text From Debris

**Goal:** make the draft less visually terrifying without making artistic decisions yet.

### Step 0.1: Mark every interruption

Use tags instead of leaving random bracket notes inside sentences.

Recommended tags:

- `[FIX]` — sentence broken, missing grammar, unclear meaning.
- `[GAP]` — missing action, transition, description, or emotional beat.
- `[ALT]` — synonym or phrasing options.
- `[FACT]` — continuity, canon, timeline, worldbuilding, or research issue.
- `[CHAR]` — character motivation, voice, emotion, or relationship issue.
- `[CUT?]` — likely unnecessary, but not decided yet.
- `[KEEP]` — rough but contains something alive.

Example:

> She reached for the cup, [ALT: trembling/shaking/unsteady] because she didn’t want him to see— [GAP: what exactly is she hiding?]

Becomes:

> She reached for the cup. `[ALT: trembling/shaking/unsteady]` `[GAP: clarify what she is hiding from him]`

You are not fixing yet. You are just moving debris out of the bloodstream of the sentence.

### Step 0.2: Create a Parking Lot

At the bottom of the document, create:

```markdown
## Parking Lot
- Nice line that may belong elsewhere:
- Possible image:
- Continuity question:
- Alternate ending idea:
- Cut but maybe reusable:
```

Move orphan ideas there instead of trying to integrate them immediately.

### Step 0.3: Color-code by problem type, not sentence length

At this stage, readability tools are premature. Track these instead:

- Red = missing information.
- Blue = word choice undecided.
- Purple = character/voice concern.
- Green = line or beat that has energy.
- Gray = probably cuttable.

**Exit condition for Pass 0:** The draft may still be ugly, but every mess has a label.

## Pass 1 — The Skeleton Pass: What Happens, Actually?

**Goal:** extract the scene’s real structure before you polish anything.

Make a scene card above the draft:

```markdown
## Scene Card
POV:
Location:
Time:
Scene type: action / argument / discovery / aftermath / seduction / reversal / setup / payoff / transition
Opening situation:
Main want:
Opposition:
Turn/change:
Ending situation:
Question pulled into next scene:
Emotional temperature at start:
Emotional temperature at end:
```

### The Five-Sentence Scene Summary

Write five plain sentences:

1. Someone wants something.
2. Something/someone resists them.
3. They try a tactic.
4. The situation changes.
5. They leave the scene altered, committed, exposed, or trapped.

If you cannot fill these in, the problem is probably not prose. It is scene design.

### The Saunders Charge Test

George Saunders often describes revision as tracking whether each beat increases or decreases the reader’s interest. For each paragraph, mark:

- `+` = increases curiosity, tension, pleasure, intimacy, dread, humor, or momentum.
- `-` = drains energy, repeats, explains too much, or delays without reward.
- `?` = unclear.

Your task is not to make every paragraph dramatic. Your task is to avoid long stretches of neutral sludge.

**Exit condition for Pass 1:** You know what the scene is doing and where it turns.

---

## Pass 2 — The Completion Pass: Fill the Holes Badly on Purpose

**Goal:** turn fragments into readable placeholders.

You are allowed to write ugly connective tissue. In fact, you should.

### Rule: Use blunt repair sentences

Instead of trying to make a missing beat beautiful, write the simplest version:

- `He was angry because she had lied.`
- `She noticed the blood on his sleeve.`
- `They both understood the conversation was over.`
- `This is where he decides not to tell her.`

Later you can dramatize or compress it. For now, you need a continuous draft.

### Replace every `[GAP]` with one of four things

1. **Action:** What physically happens?
2. **Information:** What must the reader know?
3. **Emotion:** What changes inside the character?
4. **Transition:** How do we get from beat A to beat B?

If you do not know which one is needed, ask: **What would confuse a reader if this stayed missing?**

### Fragment repair method

For unfinished sentences, do this:

1. Copy the fragment.
2. Ask: what was the sentence trying to accomplish?
3. Complete it in the plainest possible way.
4. Do not polish.

Example:

> He opened the door and saw—

Possible repairs:

- `He opened the door and saw Mara standing in the rain.`
- `He opened the door and saw that the room had been emptied.`
- `He opened the door and saw exactly why she had been afraid.`

Choose based on scene function, not prettiness.

**Exit condition for Pass 2:** Someone could read the draft from start to finish without falling into a hole.

---

## Pass 3 — The Triage Pass: Salvage, Rewrite, Cut, or Move

**Goal:** stop line-editing sentences that should not exist.

For each paragraph or beat, assign one label:

- **S = Salvage:** the beat works; improve the prose.
- **R = Rewrite:** the idea is needed, but the current wording fights you.
- **C = Cut:** the beat does no necessary work.
- **M = Move:** the beat belongs elsewhere.

### Rewrite if:

- You have tried to fix the sentence three times and it is still awkward.
- The sentence contains a good idea trapped in bad syntax.
- The emotional logic is right but the surface action is wrong.
- The sentence exists only because of how you drafted, not because of what the reader needs.
- You are preserving it because you suffered to write it.

### Cut if the sentence does none of these jobs:

A sentence should usually do at least one job, and strong sentences often do two or three.

- Advance action.
- Reveal character.
- Change emotional pressure.
- Deliver necessary information.
- Establish or alter atmosphere.
- Create rhythm or emphasis.
- Set up/pay off something.
- Sharpen voice.

If a sentence only says what the reader already knows, cut or combine it.

### Keep even an ugly sentence if:

- It contains a specific observation.
- It reveals desire, fear, shame, contradiction, or power.
- It has a rhythm that fits the character.
- It creates an image you cannot easily replace.
- It changes the reader’s understanding of the scene.

### The “Blank Page Rewrite” Test

If a paragraph feels unsalvageable, do not delete it immediately. Below it, write:

```markdown
What this paragraph must do:
1.
2.
3.

New version:
```

Rewrite from function, not from wording.

Then compare. Keep whichever version performs the job better.

**Exit condition for Pass 3:** Every paragraph has a reason to remain.

## Pass 4 — Character Authenticity Pass

**Goal:** make characters feel internally alive instead of like plot delivery devices.

For each major character in the scene, fill this out:

```markdown
Character:
Surface goal in this scene:
Hidden need/fear:
What they are not saying:
What they misunderstand:
What they are trying to control:
What would embarrass them if exposed:
How they protect themselves:
How they show emotion indirectly:
What changes for them by the end:
```

### The Three-Layer Dialogue Test

Good dialogue usually has three layers:

1. **Text:** what they literally say.
2. **Tactic:** what they are trying to do with the words.
3. **Leak:** what slips out despite them.

Example:

> “You could have called.”

Text: You did not call.
Tactic: Accuse / invite apology.
Leak: I waited for you.

If your dialogue is flat, usually layer 2 or 3 is missing.

### Character depth diagnostic

A character is probably too thin if:

- They only react in the most obvious way.
- They explain themselves too clearly.
- They never protect their pride.
- They never misread another person.
- They never want two incompatible things.
- Their body language always matches their dialogue.
- Their choices serve the plot but not their psychology.

### Add contradiction, not backstory

Instead of adding a paragraph of history, add a contradiction in behavior.

- They want comfort but reject kindness.
- They are brave in public and cowardly in intimacy.
- They insult someone while making them tea.
- They claim not to care and remember every detail.

Contradiction creates depth faster than explanation.

### Fanfiction-specific character check

For canon characters, ask:

1. What would this character never say directly?
2. What emotion do they disguise as another emotion?
3. What is their default defense mechanism?
4. What canon behavior am I echoing without copying?
5. What situation in this story pressures them differently than canon?

Authenticity does not mean repeating canon lines. It means preserving the character’s emotional grammar under new pressure.

**Exit condition for Pass 4:** Each main character wants something, hides something, and changes tactics.

## Pass 5 — Word Choice and Synonym Pass

**Goal:** choose words by function, not by decorative preference.

Do not ask, “Which synonym is prettiest?” Ask, “What job does this word need to do?”

### The Synonym Decision Ladder

For every `[ALT]`, choose by this order:

1. **Meaning:** Which word is most accurate?
2. **POV:** Which word would this narrator/character notice or use?
3. **Register:** Is the diction too formal, too modern, too poetic, too blunt?
4. **Sound:** Does it fit the rhythm of the sentence?
5. **Image:** Does it create the right physical or emotional picture?
6. **Freshness:** Is it less expected without being distracting?
7. **Continuity:** Have you overused this word nearby?

Example:

`[ALT: trembling/shaking/unsteady]`

- `trembling` = delicate, fear, cold, restrained emotion.
- `shaking` = plainer, stronger, more physical.
- `unsteady` = broader; could be emotional, drunk, tired, injured.

Choose based on what the reader should infer.

### Word choice by emotional temperature

- In panic: shorter, concrete words.
- In grief: sensory fragments, absences, repetition.
- In desire: attention narrows; physical detail intensifies.
- In shame: evasions, self-corrections, displacement.
- In anger: hard verbs, clipped rhythm, fewer qualifiers.
- In dissociation: distance, passivity, abstract phrasing, odd details.

### The Le Guin Sound Pass

Ursula K. Le Guin emphasized the sound and rhythm of prose. Read important paragraphs aloud or in a whisper. Mark places where your mouth trips.

Fix not only meaning, but breath.

**Exit condition for Pass 5:** All synonym piles are resolved or deliberately deferred.

---

## Pass 6 — Prose Compression and Expansion Pass

**Goal:** decide where to cut, where to linger, and where to sharpen.

### Use the accordion method

For each scene beat, decide whether to:

- **Compress:** routine action, repeated thought, obvious transition, known information.
- **Expand:** choice point, emotional reversal, sensory discovery, intimacy, danger, reveal.

A common draft problem: expanding logistics and compressing feelings. Reverse that.

### Sentence cut test

Cut a sentence if the surrounding paragraph reads better without it.

Do this physically:

1. Duplicate the paragraph.
2. Remove the sentence.
3. Read both versions.
4. If nothing important is lost, cut.
5. If only one word/image is needed, transplant that into another sentence.

### The “because” test

For emotionally important actions, ask:

> Can I explain why this character does this without inventing a reason outside the text?

If not, add motivation earlier or change the action.

### The “camera and pulse” test

For every paragraph, identify:

- **Camera:** what the reader sees/hears/senses.
- **Pulse:** what the moment means emotionally.

If a paragraph has only camera, it may feel flat.
If it has only pulse, it may feel abstract.

**Exit condition for Pass 6:** The story’s attention is proportional to importance.

---

## Pass 7 — Final Polish Pass

**Goal:** make the draft smooth without sanding off personality.

Only now use readability tools and sentence-length plugins.

### Final checks

- Remove leftover tags.
- Search for repeated pet words.
- Check paragraph openings.
- Check dialogue attribution.
- Check pronoun clarity.
- Check timeline and continuity.
- Read the first and last sentence of every scene.
- Read aloud one page.
- Export or save as the clean second/polished draft.

### Do not over-polish into neutrality

A weird but precise sentence is often better than a flawless bland one.

**Exit condition for Pass 7:** The reader no longer sees the scaffolding.

# Fast Version: The 90-Minute Rescue for a 3,000-Word Draft

Use this when you are overwhelmed.

## 0–10 min: Label the mess

- Mark `[GAP]`, `[ALT]`, `[FIX]`, `[CHAR]`, `[CUT?]`.
- Move random notes to Parking Lot.

## 10–20 min: Scene card

- Fill in want, opposition, turn, ending state.

## 20–45 min: Fill holes badly

- Replace gaps with plain sentences.
- Complete fragments.
- Do not polish.

## 45–60 min: Triage

- Label paragraphs S/R/C/M.
- Cut obvious repetition.
- Rewrite only paragraphs marked R.

## 60–75 min: Character and dialogue

- Add what each character is hiding.
- Make dialogue tactical, not merely informative.

## 75–90 min: Resolve synonyms and smooth

- Use the Synonym Decision Ladder.
- Read key paragraphs aloud.
- Leave remaining issues tagged for next session.

# Anti-Paralysis Rules

## 1. Never open the draft and “start editing.”

Always open with a named pass.

Bad:

> I’ll edit chapter 4.

Better:

> I’ll do Pass 2: fill all `[GAP]` notes with ugly complete sentences.

## 2. Make the next action mechanical

When stuck, choose one:

- Find all brackets.
- Complete five fragments.
- Resolve ten synonym notes.
- Mark every paragraph S/R/C/M.
- Write the scene card.
- Cut three sentences that repeat information.

## 3. Use ugly brackets intentionally

Your draft is not failing because it has brackets. It is failing only if the brackets never get processed.

## 4. Work in copies named by function

Example:

- `Story_v1_raw`
- `Story_v2_legible`
- `Story_v3_scene`
- `Story_v4_character`
- `Story_v5_polished`

This makes progress visible.

## 5. Separate judgment from labor

Some sessions are judgment sessions: decide what works.
Some are labor sessions: apply decisions.
Do not demand both every time.

# Author-Derived Practices Worth Stealing

These are not rules, but useful lenses.

## George Saunders: track the reader’s energy

Ask after every beat: did this make the reader want the next line more or less?

## Ursula K. Le Guin: prose is also sound

Meaning is not enough. Read aloud for rhythm, emphasis, breath, and music.

## John Steinbeck: write to one person

When voice gets stiff, imagine telling the story to one ideal reader rather than performing for an abstract audience.

## Zadie Smith: change your relationship to the draft

Distance helps. If possible, leave time between drafting and final judgment. If not, create artificial distance by changing font, layout, or device.

## Francine Prose: read like a writer

When stuck, take one paragraph by an author you admire and identify what each sentence is doing. Then return to your own paragraph with that same practical attention.

## Verlyn Klinkenborg: sentences are units of perception

Do not think of sentences only as grammar. Ask: what does this sentence let the reader perceive that they could not perceive before?

# Decision Rules Cheat Sheet

## Completely rewrite when:

- The function is clear but the wording is tangled.
- You are preserving the sentence out of sunk cost.
- The sentence has more patches than original fabric.
- The POV, emotion, or action has changed since drafting.
- The paragraph’s order is wrong.

## Cut when:

- It repeats known information.
- It explains an emotion already dramatized.
- It delays the scene without increasing tension, intimacy, dread, humor, beauty, or curiosity.
- It exists only to transition between two stronger sentences.
- It protects the reader from ambiguity they could productively feel.

## Keep when:

- It is specific.
- It creates pressure.
- It reveals contradiction.
- It sounds like the POV.
- It contains an image, rhythm, or insight you cannot replace.

## Expand when:

- A character makes a meaningful choice.
- A relationship changes.
- A secret is almost revealed.
- The reader needs to feel the cost.
- The moment is the reason the scene exists.

## Compress when:

- Characters move through routine logistics.
- You are explaining what readers can infer.
- The beat only gets us from one place to another.
- You have multiple sentences with the same emotional value.

# The Most Important Rule

Do not ask an ugly draft to be beautiful.

Ask it to become **legible**.
Then **functional**.
Then **alive**.
Then **beautiful**.
